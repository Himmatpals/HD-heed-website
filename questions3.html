General Guidelines for NDIS Intake Calls

Purpose of the Call: The initial phone call with a potential client (or their representative) serves as both a screening and onboarding conversation. Your goal is to determine if and how you can meet the caller's needs, explain your services, and guide them toward the next steps. Keep the call professional yet warm – think of it as the first step in building a supportive relationship, not a rushed transaction.

Key Principles:

  • Empathy and Active Listening: Start by warmly greeting the caller, introducing yourself and your organization. Listen actively to their story or inquiry without interruption. Acknowledge their feelings or any challenges they mention (e.g. "I understand that arranging support can be stressful, and I'm here to help make it easier."). Establishing trust from the first moments is critical, especially in disability support contexts.
  • Clarity and Simplicity: Use clear, jargon-free language. Many callers may not be familiar with all NDIS terms, so explain concepts when needed (e.g. what a "service agreement" or "support coordinator" is). Avoid overwhelming them with too much detail upfront – focus on the essentials the caller needs to know right now.
  • Person-Centered Approach: Every participant's situation is unique. Tailor the conversation to their needs and goals. Ask open-ended questions to understand what support they are looking for and what matters most to them (e.g. "Can you tell me a bit about what you're hoping to achieve with support?"). This not only provides you information but also empowers the caller to voice their priorities.
  • Gather Key Information: Have an intake checklist handy. Early in the call (after a bit of rapport), gently collect important details:
    • Participant's Name, Age, and Disability (for context of needs).
    • Caller's Relationship to participant (self, family member, coordinator, etc.). Ensure you have consent to discuss details if it's not the participant themselves.
    • NDIS Status: Do they have an NDIS plan already? If so, confirm basics like NDIS number (if they have it on hand), how their plan is managed (Agency/NDIA-managed, Plan-managed, or Self-managed), and key support areas they have funding for. This helps you tailor your responses (for example, if they are NDIA-managed, you'll reassure that you are a registered provider able to claim through the portal).
    • Location and Service Needs: Where do they live and what supports are they interested in (e.g. personal care, community access, nursing, therapy, etc.)? Check if it's within your service region and scope.
    • Current Situation: Are they starting services for the first time, transitioning from another provider, or needing urgent support (like post-hospital discharge)? Note any time constraints or special circumstances.
  • Explain Services and Value: Give a concise overview of your organization and how you can help. Mention that you are an NDIS Registered Provider, which means you meet strict quality and safety standards. Highlight key services you offer (tailored to what they need – for example, if they mentioned needing nursing care, emphasize your qualified nursing staff). If relevant, note your experience or specialties (e.g. "We have been supporting NDIS participants since 2018 and have experienced staff for high-care needs"). Reinforce what sets you apart (such as person-centered planning, reliable staffing, good communication, or any awards/credentials as evidence of quality).
  • Invite Questions and Concerns: Throughout the call, encourage the caller to ask any questions. Be prepared to answer common FAQs. Address concerns truthfully and positively, framing answers in a "Yes, we can help with that" manner whenever possible. If something is outside your scope, explain what you can do or suggest a resource.
  • Next Steps: End the call with a clear plan. This might include:
    • Scheduling a follow-up assessment or face-to-face meeting.
    • Outlining the intake paperwork or service agreement signing process.
    • Explaining any necessary coordination (e.g. you might say "Our next step would be to draft a Service Agreement and support plan. I can email that to you by tomorrow, and we can review it together.").
    • Providing your contact info and reassuring them they can call back with any further questions. If the participant has a support coordinator or other key contact, note whether you'll reach out to them or if they should.
  • Tone and Professionalism: Maintain a friendly, caring tone. Use the caller's name and the participant's name (if different) during the call. Keep notes of important details as you talk. Before hanging up, thank them for contacting you and reinforce that you're looking forward to supporting them.

Remember, the initial phone call often leaves a lasting impression – you want the person to feel heard, informed, and confident that your service will meet their needs.

Standard New Participant Inquiry

Scenario Overview: A participant themselves (or their close supporter) calls for the first time to inquire about services. They likely have an NDIS plan already (or approval pending) and are shopping around for providers. This is the most common intake scenario – essentially a general intake call.

Special Considerations: These callers might ask broad questions about your services, how everything works, and why they should choose your organization. They may be nervous or unsure about the process if they're new to NDIS. Be patient and informative, giving a good first impression of a well-organized, caring provider.

Checklist/Steps:

  1. Warm Greeting: "Hello, you've reached [Your Organization], this is [Your Name]. How can I help you today?"
  2. Understand Needs: Ask what supports they are looking for and listen. Gently probe for context (living situation, any immediate concerns).
  3. Gather Details: Confirm NDIS plan status and management, basic personal details, location.
  4. Explain Suitability: If you can provide what they need, assure them ("Yes, we do have support workers in your area who can help with daily living tasks"). If any request is outside your service, explain alternatives (perhaps refer them to another service or offer partial help).
  5. Cover Process: Describe how onboarding works – e.g., you will set up a meeting, create a service agreement, and can usually start services within a certain timeframe. Emphasize person-centered planning (you'll tailor supports to their goals).
  6. Invite Questions: "Feel free to ask any questions at all – I know it's a lot of information."
  7. Close Positively: Summarize next steps, thank them for reaching out, and express enthusiasm to work with them.

Common Questions & Ideal Answers (Standard Inquiry):

Q: What services do you provide under NDIS?
A: We offer a range of NDIS-funded supports. Our core services include assistance with daily personal activities (such as help with showering, dressing, meal prep), domestic assistance (household tasks), community participation (support to go out for shopping, appointments or social activities), and nursing care for any health-related needs. We tailor services to each person – during our initial assessment we'll discuss exactly what support you want and create a plan around your goals.
Q: Are you a registered NDIS provider?
A: Yes, we are fully NDIS Registered. This means we have met the government's strict quality and safety standards for providers. Being registered also means if your plan is NDIA-managed, we can make service bookings and claims directly through the NDIS portal. You can have confidence that we adhere to all the NDIS Practice Standards – in other words, you're in safe and qualified hands.
Q: How much experience do you have? How do I know I can trust your service?
A: [Your Organization] has been supporting people under the NDIS since [Year]. We have extensive experience working with participants of all ages and needs – from basic daily support to very high care needs. Many of our team members have years (even decades) of experience in disability support. We pride ourselves on quality – for example, we undergo regular audits to maintain our NDIS registration and even have additional accreditation (like ISO 9001 for quality management) to prove our high standards. We're happy to share testimonials or connect you with existing clients who can speak to our reliability and care. Ultimately, we let our track record speak – our focus is building trust by delivering on what we promise.
Q: Who are your staff? Are they qualified?
A: Our support staff come from a range of professional backgrounds in disability and community care. Every support worker undergoes thorough training to ensure they can provide respectful, safe, and empowering support. We provide ongoing training beyond the basic certifications – for instance, our nurses give hands-on workshops to support workers on things like using hoists safely or managing a feeding tube. All staff have cleared relevant background checks (like police checks and Working With Children Checks where needed). We also try to match staff to participants thoughtfully – if you have any preferences (like you'd prefer a female support worker, or someone who speaks a certain language), we'll do our best to accommodate that. Our goal is to have a skilled team member that you feel comfortable with.
Q: What areas do you service?
A: We currently provide services across [list regions, e.g., "the greater Melbourne area focusing on the northern and western suburbs"]. (If applicable: We also have staff in [Other Region]). If you're unsure whether we cover your suburb, let me know where you are – I'll confirm. (If out of area) If it turns out you're outside our service area, I can help refer you to a provider closer to you.
Q: How soon can you start services?
A: We understand many people need support as soon as possible. Our intake process can be quite quick. Typically, after this call, we would arrange to meet you (or have another detailed chat) to develop a Service Agreement and support plan. We can often begin services within [X] days of signing the agreement – sometimes sooner, depending on staff availability and your schedule. If there's urgency (for example, you have no current support or you're being discharged from hospital), please let us know – we will prioritize getting things in place quickly. We've handled urgent starts before successfully.
Q: What's the process to get started?
A: The process is straightforward:
  • Initial Assessment: We'll schedule a time (either a phone call or face-to-face meeting) where we do a more detailed assessment of your needs and goals. This helps us create a tailored support plan.
  • Service Agreement: We'll put together a Service Agreement – this is a document that outlines what supports we'll provide, the rates (which follow the NDIS price guide), and terms like the notice required if you cancel a service. We'll review it with you to make sure you're happy with everything. Once you sign the agreement, we can lock in a start date.
  • Staff Matching: We select a suitable support worker (or team of workers) based on your preferences and needs. We can arrange an introduction with them before regular supports begin, if you like.
  • Commencement: Services begin as scheduled. We'll check in with you frequently in the early days to ensure things are going well. And of course, you can contact us anytime with questions or adjustments.
Throughout, we aim to keep things simple and transparent. We handle any NDIS admin (service bookings, documentation) in the background so you can focus on the support itself.
Q: Do I have to sign a Service Agreement?
A: While it's not legally mandatory, the NDIS strongly encourages having a written Service Agreement with your provider – and we find it's best practice for clarity. The Service Agreement protects you because it clearly states what we will deliver and any terms of service. It also covers things like how you or we can end the service if needed. We keep our agreements very straightforward and in plain language. We will never lock you in indefinitely – you remain in control and can change or cancel the agreement if your needs or preferences change (we just ask for a notice period, usually 2 weeks, as is standard). We're happy to go through the agreement line by line with you to ensure you're comfortable.
Q: How are services billed and paid?
A: This depends on how your NDIS plan is managed:
  • If you're NDIA-managed (Agency-managed): We will create a service booking in the NDIS MyPlace portal for the supports you'll use with us. After we deliver services (e.g. each week), we claim payments directly from your NDIS funding. You don't have to pay us out of pocket – it's all handled through the portal.
  • If you're Plan-managed: We will send our invoices to your Plan Management provider. They pay us on your behalf from your NDIS funds. We are very familiar with working with plan managers and will coordinate with them for smooth payments.
  • If you're Self-managed: We will send invoices to you (usually weekly or fortnightly). You can then pay us directly (we accept bank transfer and other common methods), and you claim the expense back from NDIS. We'll make sure the invoices have all the info you need to do the reimbursement easily.
In all cases, the rates we charge are per the NDIS Pricing Arrangements (price guide), so no out-of-pocket costs for you except if you deliberately choose extra services not covered by NDIS (which is rare and we'd discuss beforehand). We'll talk about the specific funding categories and ensure you have enough budget for the services – part of our job is to help you manage the funding so it lasts the plan duration.
Q: Do you charge any additional fees or travel costs?
A: We follow the NDIS pricing rules on any additional charges. For example, if a support worker needs to travel to your home or community location, NDIS allows providers to charge a travel fee (usually the time or distance equivalent of the trip) – but within strict limits. We will be transparent about any such charges: they would all be clearly listed in the Service Agreement and come out of your NDIS funds, not your pocket. We do not have hidden fees like "administration fees" or sign-up fees. Everything is covered by your NDIS funding as per the price guide. If you have specific concerns (like you live in a rural area and worried about travel costs), let's discuss and we can estimate those for you.
Q: What is your cancellation policy?
A: Our cancellation policy is aligned with the NDIS policy. The NDIS requires 7 clear days' notice to cancel a scheduled support without charge. This means if you cancel a service with less than 7 days' notice, we are permitted to charge the cost of that service (since we've set aside the time/staff for you). However, we understand emergencies happen – we encourage you to let us know as soon as possible if you need to cancel or reschedule, and we'll be as flexible as we can. (If your organization offers a more lenient policy, mention it: For instance, our organization only requires 24 hours notice for most supports – which is more flexible than the NDIS rule.) We will clearly explain the cancellation terms in the Service Agreement as well. The bottom line: if you wake up sick one day or something urgent comes up, just inform us and we'll try to work with you to avoid unnecessary charges.
Q: What if I start services and then decide I want to stop or switch provider?
A: You are always in control of your supports. If you decide our service isn't the right fit or your circumstances change, you have the right to end the Service Agreement. We ask for [X] days' notice (common is 14 days) in the agreement. This notice allows us to appropriately close out your services and for you to line up other supports. The NDIS gives participants full freedom to change providers – you don't even have to give a reason if you don't want to. We'll respect your decision and even help with a transition if needed (for example, we can provide copies of support notes or handover information to your new provider, with your consent). Our priority is that you get the support you need, even if it ends up being with someone else.
Q: Why should I choose your service over others? (Often unspoken, but it's in the caller's mind)
A: (This is your opportunity to summarize your value proposition.) We believe what sets [Your Org] apart is our commitment to person-centered care and reliability. We are not a huge impersonal company – when you join us, you get a small, dedicated team that knows you well. You'll have a consistent point of contact (like a Care Coordinator) for any questions, and we really get to know your preferences. We also have clinical oversight – for instance, our services are clinically led (our care managers include registered nurses who ensure even complex needs are safely supported). In short, we combine the resources of a professional organization with the heart of a family business. Our current clients often commend us on being responsive and going the extra mile. Ultimately, we're here to empower you to live your best life – and we'd be honored to be your chosen support provider.
Q: Okay, what are the next steps if I want to proceed?
A: Great! The next step would be [schedule an in-person meeting / a longer intake call] so we can develop a detailed plan together. We'll also prepare a Service Agreement for you to review. How about we set up a meeting on [proposed date/time]? If that works, I'll come to you (or we can meet at our office/your home/coffee shop – wherever you're comfortable) and we can go through everything. I will also send you an email today with my contact details, a summary of what we discussed, and the list of any documents that might be useful to have (for example, a copy of your NDIS plan or OT reports, if available – but no rush). Feel free to call or email me anytime if you think of more questions. We're excited to work with you and make sure you get the support that suits you well!

Family Member or Advocate Inquiry

Scenario Overview: Here, a family member, guardian, or advocate of the participant is the one calling. For example, a parent might call about services for their adult child, or a son/daughter calls on behalf of an elderly parent with disability. In some cases, the participant might not be able to speak for themselves easily (due to communication difficulties), or they might simply prefer their loved one handle inquiries.

Special Considerations: When a third party is calling, you have to balance including them and respecting the participant's autonomy. Always ascertain whether the participant is aware of this inquiry and has given permission (especially if discussing personal details). Family callers often have their own concerns: they want to ensure their loved one will be safe, well-cared for, and respected. They may ask a lot of detailed questions and can sometimes be anxious or protective – this is natural. Show empathy for their perspective ("I understand you want the very best for [Participant], and we do too."). If appropriate, encourage that the participant be involved in planning as much as possible (even if they can't call themselves, maybe they can attend a meeting or at least decisions are made in their interest).

Checklist/Steps:

  1. Establish Authority Early: "May I ask, are you calling on behalf of an NDIS participant? What is your relationship to them?" If the participant isn't present, ensure that it's okay to proceed. (If the family member is a legal guardian or nominee, note that. If not, you might only share general info until the participant can consent to specifics.)
  2. Show Respect for Participant: Use the participant's name often. Emphasize that your service is person-centered – even though you value the family's input, the participant's preferences will be central.
  3. Address Family's Key Worries: Commonly, family will ask about trustworthiness of staff, consistency, communication channels with family, etc. Provide reassurance on those fronts (e.g. staff checks, experience, how you'll keep family in the loop with permission).
  4. Gather Info: Get the participant's details and needs (often the family member will eagerly describe the situation). Listen to any red flags or special requirements they mention (like behavioral issues, medical needs, routines that are important).
  5. Plan Next Steps with Inclusion: Suggest meeting with the participant and family if possible. Families appreciate being included in discussions. If the participant is non-verbal or not decision-capable, note who the decision-maker is (e.g. parent for a minor, plan nominee for an adult, guardian, etc.).
  6. Boundary Setting: If needed, gently clarify privacy boundaries. For instance, if a capable adult participant is not present, you might say you will need the participant's consent to proceed further into personal planning. Offer to schedule a 3-way call or meeting. Most families will understand that you have to ultimately get the participant's agreement.

Common Questions & Ideal Answers (Family Calls):

Q: I'm calling for my [son/daughter/parent/etc]. They have an NDIS plan. Can I arrange services for them?
A: Yes, absolutely – we often work with family members or advocates during the intake process. First, may I have your name and your relationship to [Participant]? (Confirm caller's authority). We're very happy to have your involvement. We will of course want [Participant] to be as involved as possible in decisions (to the extent they're comfortable), but we know how vital family support is. We can definitely discuss and start arrangements through you. Many of our best outcomes come from collaborating closely with families.
Q: My [relative] isn't very communicative / has intellectual disability. How will you ensure they're looked after properly?
A: We have experience supporting clients who have communication difficulties or cognitive impairments. Our approach is to get to know how your family member communicates – whether it's through certain behaviors, non-verbal cues, or using assistive communication devices. We often consult with family to learn the individual's likes/dislikes, routines and triggers. Every support plan is very personalized. We also implement regular care notes and communication logs – so after each visit, we record what was done and how [Participant] was, which can be shared with you if you wish. Regarding safety: all our staff are trained to be attentive and gentle, and to watch for any signs of discomfort or issues. If something seems off, we address it immediately and inform the appropriate contacts. We really treat each participant with the same care and caution we'd want for our own family.
Q: Will I be kept informed about what's going on?
A: With the participant's consent (and assuming they're okay with us discussing their support with you), yes, we can keep you in the loop regularly. We can arrange updates in a way that works for everyone – for example, a brief weekly email or a quick call to you, or even sharing access to our client portal if we have one. At the outset, we'll ask [Participant] and you about communication preferences. Many families appreciate being informed of progress or any issues, and we're happy to do that. We just balance it with the participant's privacy and independence; we find most participants welcome their family being involved, but we always double-check. If at any point you have questions or concerns, you'll also have our contact and you can reach out anytime. You should never feel left in the dark.
Q: What if my family member doesn't want support on a given day, or refuses the worker?
A: We understand that can happen. If [Participant] is having a day where they refuse service, our staff are trained not to force anything. We'll try gentle encouragement or offer alternate activities, but ultimately we respect the person's wishes. We'd then communicate with you (and the NDIS support coordinator if involved) to adjust the plan if needed. As for not liking a particular support worker: we take careful matching steps to avoid this, but if it does occur, please let us know immediately. We will not be offended – we will simply assign someone else who might be a better fit. Participant choice is paramount; they have the right to say who comes into their home. We maintain a roster of diverse staff, so we can usually find the right match after getting feedback.
Q: Are your support workers trustworthy and qualified? How do you vet them?
A: All our support workers go through rigorous screening. We do nationwide police checks, reference checks, and they must have up-to-date Working With Children Checks (for minors) and other relevant screenings. We verify their qualifications and past experience. Once they join us, as I mentioned, we also provide ongoing training and supervision. We often introduce a new support worker to a client gradually – for example, a meet-and-greet visit first – so you and [Participant] can get comfortable. If you ever have any concern about a staff member, we would act on that immediately. Our service is also fully insured and adheres to the NDIS Code of Conduct, which all workers are oriented to. You can have peace of mind that we have high standards for whom we hire.
Q: Will the same person come every time? We really want consistency for [Participant].
A: We also believe consistency is very important. We aim to allocate a primary support worker (or a small team of two for backup) to each participant, rather than a rotating cast of strangers. So yes, in general, the same person will come to each scheduled shift. If that primary worker is sick or on leave, we would inform you and provide a suitable substitute – but we'll always discuss it with you first whenever possible. Our goal is that [Participant] builds a trusting relationship with their support staff, and that can only happen with continuity. Over time, if your needs increase, the team might grow, but we keep it as consistent as possible.
Q: How do you handle medications or special medical needs?
A: We have trained staff for various health-related supports. If your family member requires medication administration, we ensure that only workers with proper medication training (and nursing oversight if needed) handle that. We keep detailed medication records. If there are specific medical tasks (like PEG feeding, catheter care, seizure management), we involve our Registered Nurse in developing the care plan and training the support workers. You can provide any existing care plans from doctors or therapists, and we'll incorporate those. Your loved one's health and safety needs will be meticulously planned for. If a nurse needs to do certain tasks directly, we can arrange nursing visits as part of the plan. Rest assured, we won't do anything we aren't qualified to do – and for what we are, we do it to a high standard of care.
Q: My parent is older (over 65) and on NDIS – will their support change or end when they age more?
A: If your parent is already an NDIS participant who entered the scheme before 65, they can continue receiving NDIS supports beyond age 65 – the NDIS won't cut them off just due to age. Many people choose to stay with NDIS because the supports are tailored to disability needs. The only caveat is if they decide to move into aged care or start using government aged care home services after 65 – that could affect NDIS eligibility. But if they're living at home and happy with NDIS, they can stick with it. We have had clients in their 70s still on NDIS plans. We might also suggest looking at My Aged Care for additional help, as sometimes they can get extra funding for things like home modifications through aged care while still on NDIS. We can guide you through those options if needed. But bottom line: turning 65 doesn't automatically stop NDIS supports, so you don't need to worry about a sudden cutoff.
Q: As a parent, I'm very nervous about letting someone into our home to care for my child. How can I be sure it's safe?
A: Your concern is completely understandable. Inviting a support worker into your home and trusting them with your child is huge. Here's what we do to ensure safety and quality:
  • We involve you in the introduction – you'll meet the worker beforehand and can be present initially during support visits until everyone is comfortable.
  • We have a strict Code of Conduct for our staff. Respecting your home, privacy, and cultural values is mandatory.
  • All staff are trained in safeguarding and professional boundaries. There's zero tolerance for any form of abuse, neglect, or even rudeness.
  • We have incident reporting mechanisms – if anything at all is amiss, our management will address it immediately. And we encourage families to give feedback anytime.
  • If it gives peace of mind, some parents install cameras in common areas or drop in occasionally – we are fine with that openness (just let us and the worker know, for transparency). Our stance is that we have nothing to hide and we want you to feel 100% confident.
Over time, as you see the worker build a positive rapport with your child and reliably show professionalism, we hope your worries will ease. We truly treat our participants like family – and by extension, we want families to feel secure and happy with our care.
Q: Do you have experience with children/young adults (or a specific condition like autism, cerebral palsy, etc.)?
A: Yes, we do. We have dedicated staff who specialize in working with [children/individuals with specific condition]. For example, many of our support workers have backgrounds in special education or have worked in pediatric settings. We also ensure all staff working with minors have Working With Children clearance and are trained in child-safety protocols. If your child has specific behavioral or sensory needs (as is common with autism, for instance), we will take extra steps: we'll ask you for insight into what strategies work best, and we can also consult with any therapists (like behavior support practitioners or OTs) involved. We really take a team approach for kids – involving parents, therapists, and teachers if applicable, so that we're consistent with routines and strategies. Our goal is not just to babysit, but to actively engage your child in meaningful activities aligned with their NDIS goals (be it improving social skills, communication, or simply having fun and giving you as parent some respite). Many parents have been delighted to see our support workers form a positive bond with their child, even those who previously struggled with new people.
Q: Will you work with other professionals involved (support coordinator, therapists, etc.)?
A: Definitely. We believe collaboration yields the best outcomes. If the participant has a Support Coordinator, we'll maintain regular contact with them (with permission) to ensure services are coordinated and the NDIS plan is used effectively. For example, we can communicate about how hours are tracking, any need for plan adjustments, or issues that arise. Similarly, if there are therapists (physio, speech, behavior support), we welcome their input on techniques we should use during support. We have attended case conferences and school meetings when invited, to represent the care side. Essentially, we're team players and will work alongside all stakeholders to support your family member holistically.
Q: How do you handle emergencies or if something goes wrong during support?
A: All staff are trained in basic first aid and emergency procedures. If there's a medical emergency, they will call 000 (ambulance) immediately and then notify you and our management. We have your emergency contact information on file. For less urgent incidents (like a minor fall, or the participant feeling unwell), the worker will use their judgement – possibly call you to pick up the participant or ask how you'd like to handle it, while ensuring the person is safe in the meantime. We also have on-call supervisors – so our staff can reach a senior manager 24/7 for guidance in a crisis. Afterwards, we document any incidents and ensure follow-up (like reviewing what happened, updating risk management plans to prevent future issues). Transparency is key – you would be informed of anything affecting your loved one's well-being right away. Thankfully serious incidents are rare, but we prepare for them so that we can respond calmly and effectively if needed.

(Maintain a supportive tone throughout. The family will likely appreciate your thoroughness and empathy.)

Support Coordinator or Professional Referral

Scenario Overview: In this case, a Support Coordinator (SC) or other professional (e.g., Local Area Coordinator, hospital social worker, case manager) calls to refer a participant to your service. Support Coordinators are professionals funded by NDIS to help participants implement their plans – they often research and coordinate providers on behalf of participants. Their calls tend to be more business-like and information-focused, since they know the NDIS system well. They might even have a standard set of questions to check if your service is a good fit.

Special Considerations: Support coordinators value efficient communication. They usually want to know: do you provide service X in location Y, do you have capacity, and how soon can you start? They'll also check specifics like your registration groups, ability to handle certain needs, and process for referrals. They might have multiple providers to call, so clear and concise answers help. However, still maintain warmth – SCs appreciate providers who are cooperative and participant-focused (and they may send future referrals if impressed). Also, SCs will often facilitate the next steps (like sending referral documents, arranging a joint meeting). Be prepared to exchange emails and formal documents promptly.

Checklist/Steps:

  1. Quick Introduction Exchange: SCs usually introduce themselves and their organization. Take note of their name and agency. Identify the participant they're referring (get the participant's name, NDIS number if offered, and basic profile).
  2. Assess Fit and Capacity: Verify that the service needed is within your offerings and that you can cover the participant's location and schedule. Be honest about capacity – if you're at full load, say so (they'll appreciate honesty to not waste time). If you can potentially take the referral, express enthusiasm and outline preliminary capacity (e.g., "We have a support worker who could do those weekday afternoon community outings in that area.").
  3. Gather Essential Info: Ask the SC for key details:
    • The participant's support needs and goals (short summary).
    • Any high-level risks or behavior considerations.
    • Funding type and budget available for this support (SCs usually know how many hours per week they want to allocate).
    • Timeline – is it an urgent start or flexible?
    • Who will be the primary contact (SC or participant or family) moving forward for scheduling the intake?
  4. Explain Onboarding Process (tailored to SC): SCs know the general drill, but clarify yours: e.g., you will want to meet participant (with SC if needed), create service agreement, etc. SC might ask you to send a service quote.
  5. Discuss Collaboration: Assure the SC you will keep them in the loop with progress and any issues (with participant consent). Support Coordinators often continue to coordinate multiple services, so they like providers who communicate.
  6. Closing: Likely, the SC will ask for your email to send referral documents or ask you to email them a quote. Exchange best contact info. Act promptly after the call on whatever you commit to (SCs often work under time pressures for their clients).

Common Questions & Ideal Answers (Support Coordinator Calls):

Q (SC): We have a participant, [Name], requiring [Service Type] in [Location]. Do you have capacity to take this on?
A: (First, get specifics) Thank you for reaching out. To confirm, you're looking for [e.g. in-home personal care, 5 mornings a week, in Suburb X]? If so, yes, we do have capacity to support that. We currently have staff in that area with the right skill set. I anticipate we could initiate services within [timeframe, e.g. "2 weeks"] once the paperwork is done. Let me gather a bit more detail to ensure a good match... (If capacity is questionable:) We are very interested in supporting [Name]. I do want to be transparent that we're quite busy in that region. I believe we can accommodate it by [adjustment or adding staff], but the start date might be roughly [date]. Is the participant's situation urgent or do we have some flexibility? (This signals honesty and sets realistic expectations.)
Q (SC): The participant has [specific needs, e.g. complex behaviors, high medical needs]. Can your team handle this?
A: We have experience with [that specific need]. For instance, we support other participants with similar [behaviors/medical requirements]. We have specialized staff and processes in place. Let me give you an overview:
  • If behavior: We have staff trained in positive behavior support and de-escalation techniques. We'd want to review the participant's behavior support plan (if one exists) or work with their psychologist/behavior practitioner. We also ensure consistency and structure, which often helps.
  • If medical: We involve our Registered Nurse in the intake. They'll do a risk assessment and train support workers on tasks like [seizure management, insulin injections, etc.]. We ensure all high-intensity supports comply with the NDIS Practice Standards.
Given what you've described, I'm confident we can meet those needs. We might request any supporting documents (like behavior support plan, nursing care plan) to prepare our team appropriately. But yes, we're equipped for this.
Q (SC): Are you registered for [specific registration group]?
A: Yes – we are registered for multiple groups. Specifically, for [Service Type], we are NDIS-registered under [Registration Group name/code]. (For example: 'We are registered for Assist Personal Activities' and 'Community Participation,' as well as nursing and therapeutic supports.') This means we can serve NDIA-managed participants in this area. If by chance something is outside our registration, let me know – but generally we cover all the core and capacity supports that our service entails.
Q (SC): The participant's plan is Agency-managed. You mentioned you're registered, so claiming will be through the portal, correct?
A: Correct. Since [Your Org] is a Registered Provider, we will handle service bookings and claims in the NDIA portal for any Agency-managed funding. You might need to provide us the NDIS number and some plan details (like end date and allocated hours for this support) so we can set up the service booking properly. We'll make sure to not exceed the budget – we can coordinate with you on the exact hours/week that fit the plan.
Q (SC): Can you give me a quick quote or breakdown of costs for X hours per week until plan end?
A: Certainly. Our rates follow the NDIS Price Guide. For [Service], the current rate is $XX per hour (assuming [weekday daytime] support). If we provide, say, 10 hours per week, that's ~$XX per week. If the plan has, for example, 6 months remaining, that would total approximately $XX for the period. I can prepare a formal service quote document with these calculations and email it to you today. The quote will include any potential additional charges (like travel if applicable, though we keep that minimal and within NDIS limits). We'll align the quote with the participant's plan dates so you can ensure it fits their budget.
Q (SC): What's your referral/ intake process from here?
A: Once you send through the referral details (you can email to [email]), we will:
  • Contact the Participant/Family: We prefer to meet the participant (and family, if appropriate) to introduce ourselves and do an initial assessment of their needs and preferences. If you or someone from your team wants to join that meeting, you're very welcome.
  • Service Agreement & Service Booking: We will draft a Service Agreement outlining the supports, schedule, and our obligations. We'll share that with the participant (and you, if participant consents, so you're in the loop). After signing, we'll create the service booking in the portal for the agreed hours.
  • Commencement: We then match a support worker and set a start date. We'll let you know when the first service is scheduled and confirm everything is in place.
Throughout, we'll keep you informed. For example, we can copy you on the service agreement email if the participant is okay with that. We find collaborative approach helpful.
Q (SC): Do you require any documents from me?
A: If you have a referral form or participant profile, that's very helpful (many SCs have a referral template – feel free to use that or I can accept an email summary). Specifically, useful information would be:
  • The NDIS plan goals (a brief idea so we can align our support).
  • Any risk assessment or support plans (behavior support plan, health care plans).
  • Emergency contacts and other providers involved.
  • Funding details: support category and budget allocated for this service.
Additionally, if the participant has a preference for support times or any cultural/language preferences, include that. We'll take all that into account. And yes, please send their NDIS number and date of birth so we can verify on the portal when making a booking.
Q (SC): How do you communicate progress or issues?
A: We can adapt to your preferred level of involvement. Typically, if there's any significant issue (missed sessions, incidents, participant not engaging, etc.), we inform the support coordinator promptly. For routine progress, we can provide updates at plan review time or monthly summaries if you need. Some SCs appreciate a quick email every month or two on how things are going – we're happy to do that. Also, if the participant's needs seem to change such that they might require a plan review (more hours, different support), we'll alert you so that you can take action on the funding side. Communication is key for us; we see support coordinators as partners in ensuring the participant succeeds. So, you can call or email me any time as well if you need an update or have a question.
Q (SC): The participant has had trouble with providers in the past (e.g. staff not turning up, poor communication). How will you be better?
A: I'm sorry to hear they've had bad experiences. We strive to be very reliable and transparent. We have an internal policy that shifts are never missed – our workers know that a no-show is unacceptable. In the rare event a staff member is ill or unavailable last-minute, our scheduler or on-call manager finds a substitute and we inform the participant (and you) immediately, before the scheduled time. We also use a scheduling system that allows us to track check-ins, so we know when a worker arrives and leaves. Communication-wise, we ensure our office is responsive. If you email or call, you will get a prompt reply (typically same day). We also assign a specific coordinator (like myself or a colleague) to oversee this participant's services, so you have a consistent contact. We've learned that consistent follow-through is what builds trust, and we take that responsibility seriously. We welcome feedback at any point, so if something isn't up to expectations, we will address it quickly. Our aim is to make your job easier by being one of the providers you can count on.
Q (SC): Great, what do you need from me to move forward?
A: You can email me at [email] with the referral information we discussed. I'll look out for it. I will acknowledge as soon as I receive it and give you an estimated timeline for next steps (like when we plan to meet the participant). If there are any other internal processes on your end, let me know. Otherwise, consider the referral accepted. I'll keep you updated as it progresses. And please, if the participant or yourself think of any other questions or adjustments, reach out anytime.

(After answering questions, confirm the plan: e.g., "So I'll await your email with details, and we'll aim to meet John early next week. I'll let you know the outcome of that meeting and when services can start." Thank them for the referral.)
