Daily Living Skills Support

Daily living skills support (sometimes called life skills training or capacity building) is a service that helps you learn and improve the skills you need for everyday independence. Instead of doing things for you, our support workers or mentors will work with you to practice and build confidence in a variety of tasks and routines. The ultimate aim is to empower you to do more on your own, making day-to-day life easier and more fulfilling.

What kinds of skills are we talking about? It can cover an array of areas – essentially anything that will help you live more autonomously. Common focuses include: personal care skills (for example, learning how to safely shower and dress yourself with minimal help, or how to groom and take care of your hygiene independently), home care skills (like cleaning your house, doing laundry, washing dishes, basic cooking, and other household chores in a way that works for you), meal preparation (planning meals, grocery shopping on a budget, cooking simple recipes – perhaps even attending a cooking class with support), money management (handling your personal finances, budgeting your NDIS funds or pension, paying bills, and using an ATM or banking app), social and communication skills (building confidence in interacting with others, making and responding to phone calls or emails, understanding social cues, and developing friendships), travel skills (using public transport, reading timetables, safely crossing roads, maybe practicing how to use rideshare or call a taxi), and time management and routine planning (learning to follow a daily schedule, use a calendar or reminder app, and structure your day). This support is very personalized – you might focus on a few key skills that align with your goals.

How the training is delivered: Life skills support can happen one-on-one in your home or community, or in a small group/workshop setting, depending on what you’re comfortable with. Often, a support worker will come and teach by doing the activity together with you. For example, they might spend an afternoon baking with you, guiding you through the recipe so you gradually learn to do it independently. Or each week you might tackle a different chore together, slowly stepping back as you get the hang of it. There’s usually a lot of hands-on practice because that’s the best way to learn. We understand that everyone learns at their own pace – our team is patient and will tailor their approach: breaking tasks into small steps, using visual prompts or checklists if that helps you, and celebrating each achievement. We also make these sessions as fun and engaging as possible.

Benefits of life skills development: The main benefit is greater independence – being able to do more things by yourself or with minimal assistance. This can have a big impact on your self-esteem and confidence. When you can take care of your own needs (even if only partially), you often feel more in control of your life and proud of what you can do. It opens up opportunities too: for example, learning to use public transport might allow you to attend a TAFE course or visit friends without needing someone to drive you. Beyond independence, life skills training also promotes safety. You learn the right way to do tasks (like safely chopping vegetables, or how to evacuate in a fire drill) which can prevent accidents. You also become more aware of your environment and how to navigate challenges. Families and carers greatly value life skills supports because it means their loved one is gaining abilities – it can lighten the load on families as you start doing things for yourself, and it prepares you for possibly living more independently in the future.

Examples of daily living skills support in action:

  • A support worker might visit in the mornings to help you establish a morning routine. At first they might guide you through steps like making your bed, brushing your teeth properly, choosing weather-appropriate clothes and getting dressed. After some time, you’ll start doing more of these steps on your own until it becomes a habit.
  • You could have a weekly session focused on cooking. One week you learn how to make a simple breakfast (like eggs on toast). Another week, you try cooking a pasta dish for dinner. The support worker teaches kitchen skills (using a knife safely, operating the oven) and each time you do a bit more independently. Eventually, you might prepare a whole meal for your family with just supervision.
  • If budgeting and shopping are goals, the support worker can help set up a budget with you – listing your income and expenses. Each fortnight when you get paid, you two can go through paying a bill together online, then take a trip to the shops to practice spending within your budget. You’ll learn to compare prices, use EFTPOS or handle cash, and keep track of change.
  • For social skills, a support mentor might accompany you to a social outing (like a local meet-up group or just out in public) and gently coach you on interactions. They might practice conversation starters with you, or how to maintain appropriate personal space, etc.
  • Using public transport could start with riding the bus with support. At first, the worker shows you how to read the timetable, which bus to catch, how to signal the driver and pay the fare. Eventually, you might ride certain familiar routes independently, knowing your support person is a phone call away if you get confused.

Remember, life skills support is highly adaptable – whatever skill you want to work on, we’ll find a way to help make it achievable for you. Even if you don’t fully master a task, learning parts of it can still make a big difference. Gaining these daily living skills not only helps you personally, but can also set you up for bigger goals like employment or living more independently. It’s an investment in your future and your confidence.
