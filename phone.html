1) Standard Participant Intake Self / Plan / Agency-Managed

Warm, structured intake for participants contacting directly. Confirms plan details, goals, risk notes, and first steps.
Purpose Quick Checklist Common Concerns & Responses Suggested Next Steps Full Conversation Script FAQs

Caller Type & Role: Intake Officer speaking directly with the participant (or plan nominee).

Context: Establish needs, goals, plan management, safety notes and booking pathway—without overwhelming.

Quick Intake Checklist

  • Confirm identity & consent; preferred name & pronouns; main contact details.
  • Plan management: self, plan-managed, or agency-managed; funding ballpark by category.
  • Goals & priorities for next 3–6 months (plain language).
  • Current supports & providers; avoid duplication; coordinate as needed.
  • Risks/safety: falls risk, allergies, behaviors, transfer notes, home access.
  • Preferences: worker gender, language, cultural notes, interests, comms mode.
  • Scheduling windows & location; transport needs; start timeline.

Common Concerns & Responses

  • “Is this covered by my plan?” We’ll map supports to your funding (Core/Capacity Building) and confirm before starting.
  • “I’m overwhelmed by NDIS.” We’ll keep things simple, explain documents line-by-line, and handle the admin with your consent.
  • “I need consistency.” We’ll prioritise a small, stable team and clear roster confirmations.
  • “Budget anxiety.” We’ll plan safe hours, monitor spending, and adjust if needed so supports last the full plan.
Suggested Next Steps
  • Propose initial schedule (e.g., 2–3hr blocks, 2–5 days/week) aligned to goals and energy levels.
  • Draft service agreement (plain English); offer to screen-share or walk through together.
  • Request any relevant notes (OT transfer techniques, risk plans).
  • Confirm communications (SMS for rostering changes, email for records), and book first visit.
  • Set 2–4 week review to fine-tune hours and match fit.

Full Conversation Script (Verbatim-Ready)

Intake: “Hi [Participant Name], I’m [Your Name] from [Organization]. Thanks for getting in touch about NDIS supports—how are you today?”

Participant: “Hi. I’m okay, thanks. I wanted to see what help I can get at home.”

Intake: “Absolutely. Before we dive in, do you prefer I call you [Preferred Name]? And are you comfortable to chat now?”

Participant: “Yes, call me [Preferred Name]. I have time now.”

Intake: “Great. First, a couple of quick checks so we set things up correctly: is your plan self-managed, plan-managed, or NDIA-managed?”

Participant: “[Plan type].”

Intake: “Thanks. And what are the main things you’d like help with over the next few months?”

Participant: “Mainly personal care in the mornings, some meal prep, and getting out once or twice a week.”

Intake: “Perfect. Do you have any current providers (like OT, physio, or a different support worker service) that we should be aware of so we coordinate?”

Participant: “I see an OT monthly, and I used to have a worker once a week but it wasn’t consistent.”

Intake: “Got it. We’ll make sure not to duplicate and will ask your permission to touch base with your OT if needed. Any safety considerations at home—falls risk, allergies, or transfer notes?”

Participant: “I use a walker and I’m a bit unsteady in the bathroom.”

Intake: “Thanks for letting me know. We’ll brief our staff with safe techniques and, if you’re happy, ask the OT for any transfer guidance. In terms of timing, which days and times suit you best?”

Participant: “Mornings are best—say 9 to 11 AM on weekdays. Outings could be Tuesdays or Thursdays.”

Intake: “That works. We’ll aim for a small, consistent team so you see familiar faces. We’ll also send your roster a week ahead and text you about any changes. For admin: we’ll prepare a simple service agreement outlining hours and costs within NDIS price limits. We can go through it together—no surprises.”

Participant: “Sounds good. I get anxious about budgets.”

Intake: “Totally understandable. We’ll plan conservative hours initially, monitor usage, and adjust if needed so your supports stretch across the plan year. You’ll stay fully in control.

Participant: “Okay. What’s next?”

Intake: “Next, I’ll propose a starter schedule (e.g., Mon–Fri mornings 2 hrs, and a 2-hr community access mid-week), email a draft agreement, and—if you agree—book your first visit. After 2–3 weeks, we’ll check in and tweak anything that’s not perfect.”

Participant: “Great. Let’s do that.”

Intake: “Brilliant. I’ll send everything today and we’ll get you started. Thanks for your time, [Preferred Name]!”

Frequently Asked Questions
  • Can you help me understand my plan? Yes—plain-language walk-throughs, and we’ll align supports to the correct categories.
  • How do bookings work? We create a service booking (if agency-managed) and confirm your roster weekly.
  • What if the match isn’t right? Tell us—no hard feelings. We’ll rematch quickly.
  • Will I run out of funding? We monitor usage and adjust hours proactively so supports last the full plan duration.

2) Family Member / Next of Kin Calling on Behalf With Participant Involved

Sensitive, clear intake when a family member is the main contact. Keeps the participant involved, honours consent, and sets up daily living supports smoothly.
Purpose Quick Checklist Common Concerns & Responses Suggested Next Steps Full Conversation Script FAQs

Caller Type & Role: Intake Officer speaking with a family member/next of kin on behalf of the participant (participant present or nearby).

Context: Confirm consent, gather needs, and design daily living/community support—while keeping the participant's voice central.

Quick Intake Checklist

  • Confirm relationship, consent, and best way to involve the participant (speakerphone, one-to-one later, etc.).
  • Plan management type & usable budgets (Core, Capacity Building; relevant line items).
  • Current providers (e.g., existing OT/Physio) to avoid duplication and coordinate.
  • Goals & immediate priorities (e.g., reduce loneliness, safe transfers, meals, outings).
  • Risks & safety: hearing difficulty, mobility weakness, transfer guidance from therapists.
  • Preferences: worker gender/language/culture; roster windows; communication channel.
  • Scheduling: preferred days/times; who confirms appointments (family vs participant).

Common Concerns & Trust-Building Responses

“Dad is hard of hearing—will he still be included?”
Yes. We’ll speak clearly, use speakerphone or captions if needed, and check in directly with him. We’ll involve him in key choices at every step.

“We don’t want to disrupt current therapy.”
We’ll keep OT/Physio as-is and focus on daily living and companionship. We coordinate with therapists to follow their guidance.

“I manage the logistics—can you contact me first?”
Absolutely. We’ll record you as the primary contact for scheduling/updates, while ensuring your dad’s consent and involvement.

“Will this fit the budget?”
We’ll plan hours within Core funding and monitor usage. If we need to adjust, we’ll do it together to protect essentials.

Suggested Next Steps
  • Propose a matched support worker (shared interests + skills) and arrange a meet-and-greet.
  • Request any OT transfer/safety notes (e.g., transfer techniques) for safe manual handling.
  • Send draft Service Agreement (plain English); offer to walk through line-by-line.
  • Book first visits (e.g., weekday midday 3h blocks) and set communication preferences (email for records, phone/text for shifts).
  • Track budget and adjust hours as needed; schedule a review after the first month.

Full Conversation Script (Verbatim-Ready)

Intake: “Hello, this is [Name] from [Your Organization]. I'm looking to speak with [Family Member Name] – I was told you're the best contact regarding [Participant Name]'s NDIS supports?”

Family Member: “Yes, that's me. I'm [Participant Name]'s daughter. Thanks for calling.”

Intake: “Thank you for reaching out on [Participant Name]'s behalf. How are you both doing today?”

Family Member: “We're doing alright. My dad (Participant) is here with me. He's a bit hard of hearing, so I tend to handle the phone conversations.”

Intake: “I understand. Hi [Participant Name] (speaking a bit louder and clearer)! I'm [Name] from [Org]. I'm talking with your daughter to make sure we arrange the supports you need. How are you today?”

Participant (in background): “Oh, hello! I'm okay, thank you.”

Family Member: “He can hear you on speaker now. He knows we're arranging some help for him.”

Intake: “Great, I'll continue to chat with both of you. So, I understand [Participant Name] has an NDIS plan, and you're helping to organize his services. Could you tell me a bit about what prompted you to contact us and what supports you're looking for?”

Family Member: “Sure. My dad had a stroke last year. He's on the NDIS and currently has some physio and occupational therapy, but we're looking to get him a regular support worker. I'm worried about him being alone most of the day when I'm at work. He could use help with meals and just someone around in case he needs anything. His speech isn't great and his right side is weak.”

Intake: “Got it. Thank you for the background. So he's already receiving physio and OT – is that through another provider or directly through NDIS?”

Family Member: “It's through another clinic. They're doing a good job with therapy, so we'd like to keep that as is. What we really need from you is more on the daily living side – kind of like in-home care and companionship.”

Intake: “Absolutely, we can cover that. We have support workers who can come to your dad's home and help with daily tasks (like preparing lunch, light housekeeping, etc.) and also just be there as a companion, maybe take him for a walk or to the local park if he's up for it. We can tailor the activities to what he enjoys and what keeps him safe.”

Participant (background): “I do get lonely sometimes, yes.”

Family Member: “He used to be very active socially, so it's been hard.”

Intake: “I understand. Social connection is so important. We'll definitely aim to match him with a support worker who shares some of his interests. For example, what does your dad like? Any hobbies or topics he enjoys?”

Family Member: “He loves gardening and watching cricket. He can't garden like he used to, but he enjoys sitting outside.”

Intake: “That's great to know. We actually have a support worker in our team, [Support Worker Name], who is an avid cricket fan and is skilled in helping with light gardening tasks. Someone like that might be a good fit to accompany your dad, talk cricket, maybe help him water the plants or just get some fresh air outside safely.”

Family Member: “Oh, that sounds promising! Dad, what do you think? Someone to talk cricket with you?”

Participant: “That would be nice.”

Intake: “Wonderful. Now, let's talk logistics for a moment. [Participant Name], your NDIS plan – do you know if it's self-managed or plan-managed, or NDIA-managed? Basically, who pays the bills from the plan?”

Family Member: “It's agency-managed. The NDIS pays providers directly. He doesn't handle any of that.

Intake: “Thanks, that means we'll just make service bookings in the system and claim payments directly. No worry there. And do you happen to know how many hours or funding is allocated for his daily support? I want to ensure we plan within his budget.”

Family Member: “He has about $20k a year in core supports available. We were thinking of starting with maybe 3 hours a day, Monday to Friday, around midday to afternoon.”

Intake: “That sounds reasonable. Rough math, that should be sustainable with $20k for quite a few months, and then plans usually renew annually. We can fine-tune it. We'll help you keep track so it doesn't overrun the budget. And if we find he needs more or less, we can adjust.”

Family Member: “Okay, great. Another thing: I usually coordinate everything for him. Is it alright if I continue to be the main contact with you? I'll make sure he's informed and involved in decisions, but it's just easier for scheduling and so on.”

Intake: “Of course. We appreciate having a dedicated family contact—it often makes communication smoother. We will involve your dad in any key discussions (since it's about him), but for day-to-day things like scheduling or updates, I'm happy to call or email you. If at any time your dad wants to speak directly with us or have a one-on-one meeting, we can do that too.”

Family Member: “Email works for me for records, but phone or text for anything urgent or scheduling is fine too.”

Intake: “Noted. I'll get all your contacts. Next, I'd like to schedule a home visit to meet you both in person. During that visit, we can introduce a potential support worker (like the cricket-fan I mentioned) to see if it's a good fit. We'll also go over a service agreement—that's a plain-English document listing what we'll do and our obligations. Would you be comfortable with that?”

Family Member: “Yes, that's fine. I'd appreciate seeing things in writing.”

Intake: “Perfect. I can email you a draft before the meeting. Is there a day next week that works for a visit? I can bring that support worker to introduce.”

Family Member: “Wednesday afternoon is good. I can be there by 3 PM.”

Intake: “Okay, Wednesday at 3 PM. Does that suit you too, [Participant Name]?”

Participant: “Yes.”

Intake: “Great! We'll be there. If you could gather any relevant therapy notes—like transfer techniques from OT—it helps our worker support your dad safely.”

Family Member: “Yes, the OT gave us a sheet. I'll have it ready.”

Intake: “Perfect. Feel free to think of any other questions. Our goal is to support your dad and make both of you feel confident and comfortable. You've done a fantastic job so far—we're here to share the load.”

Family Member: “Thank you. I'm hopeful this will help him and me.”

Intake: “I'm sure it will. I'll send an email now with my contact info, the draft service agreement, and the appointment confirmation. Don't hesitate to reach out if anything comes up before then.”

Family Member: “Will do. Thanks for your help today.”

Participant: “Thank you.”

Intake: “You're both very welcome. Take care, and see you soon.”

Frequently Asked Questions
  • Can I be the main contact? Yes. We'll list you as primary for scheduling/updates and always involve the participant in key decisions.
  • How do you respect consent? We confirm the participant's consent at intake and before key changes; we can also arrange direct check-ins with them.
  • Will you coordinate with current therapists? Absolutely—aligned with existing OT/Physio, and we'll request safety notes (e.g., transfers).
  • How do we manage the budget? We propose hours within Core funding, create service bookings (if agency-managed), and monitor usage to avoid overspend.
  • Can you match interests (e.g., cricket, gardening)? Yes—matching shared interests helps rapport and combats loneliness. We'll try to find a great fit.
  • Communication preferences? Email for records; phone/text for urgent rostering. Tell us what works best for you.

3) Support Coordinator Referral SC / LAC-Led Intake

A collaborative intake when a Support Coordinator (or LAC) refers a participant. Keep it simple, safe, and fast: align goals, budgets, risks, and reporting from day one.
Purpose Key Info to Gather Common Concerns & Responses Suggested Next Steps Full Conversation Script FAQs

Caller Type & Role: Intake Officer (admin/clinical) speaking with the participant and their Support Coordinator (SC) via 3-way call or speakerphone.

Context: The SC has outlined needs and funding. Your job: confirm scope, avoid duplication, set a clear start plan, and agree on comms & review rhythm.

Key Information to Gather

  • Consent to coordinate with SC and other providers (confirm verbally + note on file).
  • Plan management type & usable budgets (Core, Capacity Building; relevant line items).
  • Goals (in participant's own words) + services SC expects you to cover (no overlap).
  • Current providers & reports available (OT/PT, PBS, discharge plans, risk plans).
  • Risks & safety: falls, transfers, allergies, behaviours, home access, emergency contacts.
  • Preferences: worker gender/language/culture; roster windows; communication channel.
  • Start target date, visit frequency/duration, reporting cadence to SC, escalation path.

Common Concerns & Responses

  • Overlap risk. We define scope, share rosters, and liaise with active providers to prevent duplication.
  • Tight funding. Start conservative, phase supports, and review monthly usage to protect essentials.
  • Risk management. Staff briefed on existing plans; clear de-escalation & incident reporting with same-day alerts.
  • Comms. Weekly quick updates + monthly review; one contact line; immediate notice of changes/incidents.
Suggested Next Steps
  • Receive referral pack (goals, budgets, risk notes, consents). If delayed, begin with a safe "starter brief".
  • Propose a starter roster (e.g., 2–3 AM supports + 1 community access) with 1–2 worker profiles for participant choice.
  • Draft Service Agreement + Schedule of Supports; offer line-by-line review (participant + SC in CC).
  • Set simple comms rhythm (weekly summary; same-day incident notifications; 4-week review booked now).
  • Create a basic crisis/escalation plan (who to call, thresholds, after-hours arrangements).

Full Conversation Script (Verbatim-Ready)

Intake: "Hi [Participant Name], hi [SC Name] — it's [Your Name] from [Org]. Do we have your consent, [Participant], to speak with [SC Name] and coordinate services together?"

Participant: "Yes, that's okay."

SC: "Consent confirmed — I've sent a signed consent as well."

Intake: "Thanks. Let's confirm your top goals and what you'd like us to deliver so we don't overlap with others."

SC: "Goals: safer morning routine; community access twice weekly; support for meal prep. OT is active. Please cover daily living + community access."

Intake: "Any safety considerations?"

SC: "Falls risk in bathroom; mild anxiety in crowds; prefers female worker. Key safe on site."

Intake: "Budget check — plan-managed, agency-managed or self-managed? What's remaining?"

SC: "Agency-managed; Core remaining ~[amount]; IDL ~[amount]. Start conservatively and review in 4 weeks."

Intake: "Proposal: Mon/Wed/Fri mornings (2.5h each) for personal care/routines, plus Tue community access (3h) — ~10.5h/week. We'll monitor spend and adjust."

Participant: "Mornings after 9am work best."

Intake: "Great. We have two matched workers (shared interests: gardening/footy). We'll send profiles for your choice. Okay for a quick meet-and-greet call tomorrow?"

SC: "Yes — we'll both join."

Intake: "We'll request OT notes and add a bathroom risk checklist to staff handover. Updates: weekly email summary; same-day incident alerts. Does that suit?"

SC: "Yes, ideal."

Intake: "Next: draft Service Agreement + Schedule of Supports; target start [date] pending your approval; book 4-week review. Anything else?"

Participant: "No, that covers it."

SC: "All good. Thanks."

Intake: "Brilliant — we'll keep it simple, safe, and within budget. Speak tomorrow for the meet-and-greet."

Frequently Asked Questions
  • How do you prevent overlap? We document scope, share rosters with active providers, and coordinate via a single update thread.
  • What if there's no rapport with the first worker? We rematch quickly — choice & control first.
  • How do you protect tight funding? Start small, phase supports, monthly usage checks, prioritise must-have routines.
  • What reporting does the SC get? Weekly summary; same-day incident alerts; 4-week review then periodic reviews as requested.
  • How fast can you start? With a "starter brief" + consent we can begin safely while fuller docs arrive.

4) Complex Needs (Behaviours / Restrictive Practices) Clinical / BSP

Specialised intake for participants with complex behavioural needs or restrictive practices. Focus on safety, dignity, and clear protocols.
Purpose Quick Checklist Common Concerns & Responses Suggested Next Steps Full Conversation Script FAQs

Caller Type & Role: Clinical Intake Specialist or Senior Coordinator speaking with participant, family, or existing support team.

Context: Establish safety protocols, confirm behaviour support plans, and ensure appropriate staffing for complex needs.

Quick Intake Checklist

  • Current Behaviour Support Plan (BSP) status and authorisation details
  • Restrictive practices in use and their approval status
  • Triggers, early warning signs, and de-escalation techniques
  • Emergency procedures and contacts
  • Staffing requirements (ratios, specific training needs)
  • Medication management protocols
  • Communication methods and preferences

Common Concerns & Responses

  • "Will staff be trained to handle difficult situations?" All staff receive comprehensive training and are matched based on experience and skills.
  • "How do you ensure consistency with our current approach?" We thoroughly review existing plans and work closely with your behaviour support practitioner.
  • "What happens in an emergency?" We have clear protocols and 24/7 clinical support for urgent situations.
  • "How is privacy maintained?" We follow strict confidentiality protocols while ensuring safety information is appropriately shared with direct support staff.
Suggested Next Steps
  • Request current BSP, risk assessments, and other relevant documents
  • Arrange a clinical handover with existing providers
  • Schedule a pre-commencement meeting with all stakeholders
  • Develop a transition plan with gradual introduction of new staff
  • Confirm emergency protocols and communication channels

Full Conversation Script (Verbatim-Ready)

Intake: "Hello, I'm [Your Name] from [Organization]'s clinical team. I understand you're looking for support for [Participant Name] who has some complex needs. How are you today?"

Family Member: "We're managing, but it's been challenging finding the right support."

Intake: "I appreciate you reaching out. To make sure we can provide the safest and most appropriate support, could you tell me about the current behaviour support arrangements?"

Family Member: "We have a Behaviour Support Practitioner who developed a plan about three months ago. There are some restrictive practices in place that are approved."

Intake: "Thank you for that information. Would you be comfortable sharing the BSP with us so we can ensure our approach is consistent?"

Family Member: "Yes, I can email it to you. The main triggers are changes in routine and sensory overload."

Intake: "That's helpful context. We'll make sure our staff are thoroughly briefed on these triggers and the de-escalation techniques outlined in the plan. Do you have specific staffing requirements?"

Family Member: "We need staff who are patient, calm, and have experience with autism and complex behaviours. Consistency is really important."

Intake: "Absolutely. We'll assign a small consistent team and arrange for them to shadow current supports before taking over. We also have a clinical team available 24/7 for guidance and emergency support."

Family Member: "That's reassuring. What about documentation and reporting?"

Intake: "We'll maintain detailed records as required and provide regular updates to you and the behaviour support practitioner. We can tailor the reporting to meet your needs."

Family Member: "Okay, that sounds good. What's the next step?"

Intake: "We'll review the BSP, then arrange a meeting with all stakeholders to develop a transition plan. We want to ensure a smooth handover that maintains stability for [Participant Name]."

Family Member: "Thank you for being so thorough. I'll send through the documents today."

Intake: "Perfect. We'll be in touch within 24 hours to schedule the meeting. Thank you for your time and trust in our service."

Frequently Asked Questions
  • How quickly can you start providing support? We typically require 1-2 weeks to properly review documents, assign appropriate staff, and develop a transition plan.
  • What qualifications do your staff have? All staff have relevant qualifications and receive ongoing training in behaviour support, de-escalation, and restrictive practices.
  • How do you handle incident reporting? We follow NDIS requirements for incident reporting and ensure all stakeholders are informed appropriately.
  • Can you work with our existing therapists? Absolutely, we believe in collaborative practice and will work closely with your existing support team.

5) High-Risk Cases (Hospital Discharge / Urgent) Urgent Response

Rapid response intake for participants in high-risk situations or needing urgent hospital discharge support.
Purpose Quick Checklist Common Concerns & Responses Suggested Next Steps Full Conversation Script FAQs

Caller Type & Role: Urgent Response Coordinator speaking with hospital staff, participant, or family member.

Context: Quickly establish needs, risks, and immediate action plan for participants in crisis or transition situations.

Quick Intake Checklist

  • Immediate risks and safety concerns
  • Discharge timeline and destination
  • Current medical status and needs
  • Equipment and environmental requirements
  • Existing supports and gaps
  • Funding status and urgent approval requirements
  • Key contacts and decision-makers

Common Concerns & Responses

  • "We need support starting tomorrow - is that possible?" Our urgent response team can often mobilize within 24-48 hours for critical situations.
  • "What if we don't have all the documentation?" We can begin with a risk assessment and provisional plan while gathering documentation.
  • "How do we ensure continuity of care?" We work closely with hospital teams to ensure smooth transition and comprehensive handover.
  • "What if the participant's needs change quickly?" We maintain flexibility and have clinical oversight to adjust supports as needed.
Suggested Next Steps
  • Immediate risk assessment and safety planning
  • Coordination with medical team regarding discharge plans
  • Rapid mobilization of appropriate staff and resources
  • Interim supports while longer-term arrangements are made
  • Urgent funding applications if required

Full Conversation Script (Verbatim-Ready)

Intake: "Thank you for calling [Organization] urgent response line. I understand this is regarding [Participant Name] who needs urgent support. Can you tell me about the situation?"

Hospital Staff: "Yes, [Participant Name] is being discharged tomorrow and has no supports in place. They have high care needs following [medical procedure]."

Intake: "I appreciate you reaching out. Let's start with the most critical needs - what are the immediate risks if support isn't in place?"

Hospital Staff: "Without support, they won't be able to manage personal care, medication, or mobility. There's a high risk of readmission."

Intake: "Understood. What's the discharge destination? Is the home environment set up appropriately?"

Hospital Staff: "They're returning to their own home. OT has recommended some equipment but it's not yet in place."

Intake: "We can help coordinate that. Let's talk about hours - what level of support is needed initially?"

Hospital Staff: "We think 4-6 hours daily for the first week, then reassess. Mainly personal care, meals, and medication prompting."

Intake: "We can arrange that. I'll need some details about funding - is there an existing NDIS plan?"

Hospital Staff: "Yes, but we're not sure about the specifics. The family can provide those details."

Intake: "Perfect. Let me connect with the family today. In the meantime, we'll start mobilizing our urgent response team. We can have staff available from tomorrow morning."

Hospital Staff: "That's a relief. What about equipment needs?"

Intake: "We have relationships with equipment providers who can prioritize urgent cases. We'll coordinate that as part of our service."

Hospital Staff: "Thank you so much. This takes a huge weight off our minds."

Intake: "It's what we're here for. I'll be the point of contact throughout this process. Let's ensure [Participant Name] has a safe transition home."

Frequently Asked Questions
  • What's your response time for urgent cases? We aim to respond within 2 hours and can often mobilize support within 24 hours for critical situations.
  • Do you work after hours and on weekends? Yes, our urgent response team operates 7 days a week including public holidays.
  • What if the participant doesn't have NDIS funding? We can help connect you with appropriate services and support applications for urgent funding.
  • How do you handle handover from hospital to community support? We have established protocols for clinical handover and work closely with hospital transition teams.

6) Nursing-Only Supports (Wound, PEG, Catheter) Clinical / Nursing

Specialised clinical intake for participants requiring nursing-specific supports such as complex wound care, PEG feeding, or catheter management.
Purpose Quick Checklist Common Concerns & Responses Suggested Next Steps Full Conversation Script FAQs

Caller Type & Role: Clinical Nurse Consultant or Senior Intake Officer speaking with participant, family, or healthcare professional.

Context: Assess clinical needs, confirm nursing requirements, and establish appropriate clinical governance for complex health supports.

Quick Intake Checklist

  • Specific clinical procedures required (wound care, PEG, catheter, etc.)
  • Current clinical status and recent changes
  • Healthcare professional involvement (GP, specialists, community nursing)
  • Medication management requirements
  • Equipment and supply needs
  • Clinical documentation available (care plans, clinical notes)
  • Emergency procedures and contacts

Common Concerns & Responses

  • "Are your nurses experienced with [specific procedure]?" Yes, we match nurses specifically to your clinical needs and ensure they have recent experience.
  • "How do you ensure quality and consistency?" We have clinical governance systems including regular audits, supervision, and care plan reviews.
  • "What happens if there's a clinical issue after hours?" We have 24/7 clinical support and clear escalation pathways.
  • "How do you coordinate with our existing healthcare team?" We establish communication protocols with your GP and specialists for integrated care.
Suggested Next Steps
  • Clinical assessment by our nurse consultant
  • Review of existing clinical documentation and care plans
  • Development of specific clinical procedures and protocols
  • Coordination with existing healthcare providers
  • Staff matching based on clinical requirements
  • Establishment of clinical governance and reporting systems

Full Conversation Script (Verbatim-Ready)

Intake: "Hello, I'm [Your Name], a nurse consultant with [Organization]. I understand you're looking for nursing support for [Participant Name]. How can we help?"

Family Member: "Yes, my father needs daily wound care for a pressure injury. The district nursing service can't meet the frequency he needs."

Intake: "I appreciate you reaching out. Could you tell me about the wound - location, stage, and current treatment?"

Family Member: "It's a stage 3 pressure injury on his sacrum. The treatment involves cleaning and packing daily."

Intake: "Thank you. Is there a current wound care plan from a wound specialist or GP?"

Family Member: "Yes, the wound clinic developed a plan last week. I can email it to you."

Intake: "That would be excellent. Our nurses will follow that plan precisely. How is [Participant Name] with the procedure?"

Family Member: "He can be anxious about pain. The nurses usually use a numbing spray first."

Intake: "We'll make sure to note that in his care plan. Do you have all the necessary supplies?"

Family Member: "We have some, but may need to restock soon."

Intake: "We can help with that - either sourcing supplies or assisting with orders. Let's talk about timing - when would you like the visits to occur?"

Family Member: "Mornings would be best, after breakfast."

Intake: "We can arrange that. I'll assign one of our experienced wound care nurses who can provide consistency. They'll do an assessment on the first visit and develop our internal care plan based on the specialist's recommendations."

Family Member: "That sounds thorough. How quickly can you start?"
