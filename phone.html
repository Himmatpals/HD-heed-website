NDIS Intake Phone Scripts — Consolidated

All scenarios in one printable page. Warm, plain English. For clinical and admin teams.

Index

1) Standard Participant Intake (Self / Plan Managed) 2) Family Member / Next of Kin 3) Support Coordinator Referral 4) Complex Needs (Behaviours of Concern / Restrictive Practices) 5) High‑Risk Cases (Hospital Discharge / Urgent Safety) 6) Nursing‑Only Supports (Wound, PEG, Catheter)

1) Standard Participant Intake (Self or Plan Managed)

Caller Type & Role Intake Officer (admin or clinical) welcoming a new participant who is self‑managed or plan‑managed.

Context: First‑contact call to gather details and explain next steps. Friendly, clear language; ensure comfort and understanding.

Key Information to Gather

  • Personal details (full/preferred name, contacts), NDIS number, plan dates.
  • Plan management type and, if plan‑managed, plan manager contacts.
  • Supports sought and goals (e.g., physio to improve mobility; daily living support).
  • Immediate needs/preferences (availability, gender preference, communication needs).
  • Support coordinator or other key contacts involved.
  • Safety/health considerations (mobility, allergies, behaviour considerations).

Common Concerns / Responses

“Will NDIS cover this — what about costs?”
Your plan funds these supports; we’ll stay within budget and invoice NDIS or your plan manager directly so you don’t handle payments.
“I’m new — what happens next?”
We’ll guide you step‑by‑step: brief intake questions now, explain services, then book an initial meeting/assessment and send a simple summary + service agreement to review at your pace.
“I have a plan manager — do I do any admin?”
We’ll coordinate invoices with your plan manager directly. You focus on the supports; we handle the admin.
“How soon can we start?”
Often within 1–2 weeks depending on availability. If you have urgent needs, tell us and we’ll prioritise.

Suggested Next Steps

  • Outline relevant services (e.g., OT, physio, support work) aligned to goals.
  • Prepare a clear service agreement; allow time for questions.
  • Book an initial assessment/meet‑and‑greet; confirm preferences.
  • Send a concise call summary with contacts and appointment details.
  • Invite ongoing questions; keep communication open.
Scripted Dialogue (Read‑ready)

Intake: Hello, this is [Name] from [Your Organization]. May I please speak with [Participant Name]?

Participant: Speaking. Hi.

Intake: Hi [Participant Name]! We received your details for NDIS supports and I wanted to welcome you and see how we can assist. Is now okay for a 10–15 minute chat?

Participant: Yes.

Intake: Great. Are you self‑managing your plan or do you have a plan manager?

Participant: I’m plan‑managed.

Intake: Thanks — we’ll invoice your plan manager directly. Could I grab their name and email? Also, what supports are you hoping to access right now?

Participant: Morning personal care daily, weekly community access, and some physio.

Intake: Perfect. We can help with all of those. I’ll note preferences and days. Could I also take your NDIS number and DOB to match our records?

Participant: [Provides details]

Intake: Thanks. Next step is an in‑person meeting or home visit. We’ll bring a simple service agreement and get you started. Would Thursday 2 PM suit you?

Participant: Yes, afternoons are best.

Intake: Done. I’ll send a summary and our contacts. If questions pop up later, reach out anytime — we’re here to help.

2) Family Member or Next of Kin Calling on Behalf

Caller Type & Role Intake Officer contacting a family member/guardian who is coordinating for the participant.

Context: Respect the family role while centring the participant’s rights, consent, and preferences. Warm, validating tone.

Key Information to Gather

  • Relationship and any formal role (guardian/nominee).
  • Participant awareness/consent to the call and ongoing involvement.
  • Participant details (name, age, NDIS number) as known.
  • Current situation and support needs from family perspective.
  • Communication challenges/preferences; who is primary contact.
  • Family goals/concerns; insights on what has/hasn’t worked.

Common Concerns / Responses

“Can I handle discussions for them?”
Yes — many families help. We’ll ensure [Participant] is comfortable and keep them central. We can formalise consent/nominee status later if needed.
“Do I need official permission?”
For intake, no formal paperwork is required to talk generally. We’ll sort any formal consents in onboarding.
“How do you handle privacy?”
We only discuss details with people [Participant] consents to. We’ll find a communication balance that respects privacy and keeps you informed.

Suggested Next Steps

  • Say hello to the participant if appropriate; plan a joint meeting.
  • Note consent/nominee needs for documentation.
  • Gather prior assessments/support plans to inform onboarding.
  • Set a clear communications plan (include family on scheduling, etc.).
Scripted Dialogue (Extended, Read‑ready)

Intake: Hello, this is [Name] from [Your Organization]. I’m looking to speak with [Family Member Name] — I was told you’re the best contact for [Participant Name]’s NDIS supports?

Family Member: Yes, that’s me — I’m [Participant]’s daughter.

Intake: Thanks for reaching out on [Participant]’s behalf. How are you both today?

Family Member: We’re okay. He’s here with me; he’s hard of hearing so I handle calls.

Intake: Hi [Participant Name]! I’m [Name] from [Org]. I’ll speak clearly and involve you both so we can arrange supports that fit you.

... [Full extended dialogue covering hobbies (gardening, cricket), companionship, plan management (agency‑managed), hours (Mon–Fri midday), family as main contact, home visit booking, safety notes, and recap.]

3) Support Coordinator Referral

Caller Type & Role Intake Officer calling a participant referred by their Support Coordinator (SC).

Context: Acknowledge the SC’s role; confirm consent to loop them in. Clarify goals, logistics, timelines, and reporting.

Key Information to Gather

  • Participant understanding of the referral/services.
  • SC name/organisation; whether to CC on updates.
  • Participant goals in their own words; alignment with SC notes.
  • Plan management, urgent timelines, review dates.
  • Other providers involved; avoid overlap; define your role.

Common Concerns / Responses

“Do I have to coordinate between you and my SC?”
No — with your permission we coordinate closely with [SC Name] so you’re not the go‑between.
“Who do I tell if I need changes?”
Tell either of us; we’ll loop each other in (with your okay). We handle service changes; SC handles plan/funding items.
“Do I have to repeat what my SC told you?”
We have notes but want your voice as well. Share only what you wish; we’ll respect that.

Suggested Next Steps

  • Review SC referral; acknowledge contact made (with consent).
  • Offer joint meeting if case is complex.
  • Agree on comms (e.g., CC SC on scheduling, monthly notes).
  • Set up service agreement and service bookings; advise SC.
  • Outline reporting for plan review.
Scripted Dialogue (Read‑ready)

Intake: Hi [Participant Name], this is [Name] from [Your Organization]. Your support coordinator, [SC Name], asked me to call — do you have a few minutes now?

Participant: Sure — she mentioned you’d ring.

Intake: Great. She noted you’re seeking OT and some daily living support. What are your top priorities in the next six months?

Participant: Showering independently and doing some cooking again.

Intake: Excellent goals. Our OT can assess home mods/equipment and techniques; a support worker can attend practice sessions. With your permission we’ll keep [SC Name] in the loop and send a short post‑assessment summary.

... [Dialogue continues with scheduling (e.g., Wed 10 AM OT), support worker Mon/Fri 2 PM, service agreement + agency‑managed service bookings, plan review report, and recap.]

4) Complex Needs (Behaviours of Concern, Restrictive Practices)

Caller Type & Role Senior Intake Coordinator / Clinical Intake Specialist speaking with participant or guardian for high‑needs case.

Context: Behaviours of concern and/or authorised restrictive practices. Non‑judgemental, safety‑first, PBS‑aligned approach. Gather triggers, strategies, PBSP details, and coordination needs.

Key Information to Gather

  • Behaviours, frequency, triggers; effective and ineffective strategies.
  • PBSP details; behaviour support practitioner contact; request copies (with consent).
  • Restrictive practices used and authorisations; ensure legal/plan alignment.
  • Staff safety requirements; training; 1:1 or 2:1; environment safety.
  • Clinician involvement (psych/psychiatry) and coordination.
  • Communication style (verbal/AAC/non‑verbal) and comprehension.
  • Support environments and sensory setup/modifications.
  • Family priorities (e.g., reduce sedation; alternative communication for anger).

Common Concerns / Responses

“Providers won’t stick around once they see behaviours.”
Our team is trained for high‑support needs, views behaviour as communication, and commits to consistency and trust — not walking away.
“Can staff cope with meltdowns or aggression?”
Yes — de‑escalation and safe crisis management training; follow plans precisely; use restrictive practices only if authorised/necessary and work to reduce them.
“PRN medication in crisis — how’s that handled?”
According to the PBSP protocol, after least‑restrictive strategies are tried. Each use prompts review to learn and prevent recurrences.
“Will they be treated with respect?”
Always — dignity, strengths focus, and rapid action if a staff attitude isn’t a fit.

Suggested Next Steps

  • Comprehensive meeting with key people; include participant as tolerated.
  • Obtain PBSP, safety plans, incidents, meds (with consent); align with clinicians.
  • Specialised training refreshers as needed; shadowing before solo shifts.
  • Assign a small, consistent team; co‑develop a crisis plan.
  • Track incident data/triggers; schedule regular reviews.
  • Offer caregiver validation and supports.
Scripted Dialogue (Read‑ready)

Intake (Clinical Specialist): Hello, [Name]? This is [Your Name] from [Your Organization] regarding [Participant]. Thanks for taking the time — this is a judgement‑free conversation so we can plan safe, effective support.

Family: He’s 20, autistic with ID; outbursts a few times a week when overwhelmed.

Intake: What helps during those moments? What makes it worse? Any PRN in an authorised plan?

Family: Space, removing throwables, talking about trains helps; PRN authorised in a PBSP; used rarely.

Intake: With consent we’ll obtain the PBSP/incident info, align with your psychologist, and focus on prevention and reducing restrictive practices. Let’s plan a calm meet‑and‑greet and start with shadowed shifts and a small, consistent team.

... [Dialogue continues with triggers (transitions, changes, sensory overload), goals, scheduling, consent, documentation, and monthly check‑ins.]

5) High‑Risk Cases (Hospital Discharge / Urgent Safety Issues)

Caller Type & Role Intake Coordinator (nurse or experienced care coordinator) calling participant/family in a high‑risk scenario.

Context: Time‑critical supports; calm, confident, reassuring tone. Rapid mobilisation and tight coordination.

Key Information to Gather

  • Nature of risk; discharge details; aftercare needs; discharge plan.
  • At‑home risks and urgency (falls, self‑harm, burnout, etc.).
  • Immediate needs for 24–72 hours (visits, overnights, equipment, meals).
  • Existing supports and gaps; avoid duplication.
  • Use of plan funds; SC/hospital liaison contacts.
  • Coordination contacts (SW/discharge planner, GP, emergency contacts).
  • Home safety (access, power, food, phone/pendant).
  • Contingency if start delayed (postpone discharge; family coverage).

Common Concerns / Responses

“I’m being discharged tomorrow — I’ll be alone.”
Worker meets you on arrival, settles you in, and checks in later. On‑call contact provided; 000 for emergencies. You will not be left unsupported.
“House isn’t ready — need equipment fast.”
We’ll coordinate urgent loan/delivery (portable ramp, shower chair) and safe interim measures while longer‑term solutions progress.
“Nights worry me — I live alone.”
Evening/morning visits, optional overnight/sleepover or on‑call checks, and pendant setup/testing to cover the night.
“It’s all too much to coordinate.”
We coordinate with hospital/SC and propose a clear Day‑1 to Day‑3 plan so you can focus on recovery.

Suggested Next Steps

  • Confirm roster/equipment urgently and share ETAs.
  • Provide a written Emergency Support Plan (who/when/what).
  • Exchange on‑call and emergency contacts (with key access).
  • Attend discharge for handover/transport if helpful.
  • Fast‑track consents/service agreement; verbal + email acceptable.
  • Set 1‑week review; give a mini “what‑to‑do” guide.
Scripted Dialogue (Read‑ready)

Intake (Nurse): Hi [Participant], this is [Name] from [Your Org] about your discharge tomorrow — may I run through supports for home?

Participant: I’m nervous about being alone.

Intake: We’ll meet you at 10 AM, travel home with you, stay several hours to settle in, do a safety check, and return at 5 PM for dinner support. We’re arranging a portable ramp and shower chair; your pendant will be tested.

Participant: Thank you — that helps.

Intake: Day 2 we’ll run morning/midday/evening visits, then taper. Community nursing is booked Mon/Wed/Fri at 11 AM; we’ll coordinate. I’ll text our on‑call number and email a simple Day‑1 to Day‑3 plan now.

6) Nursing‑Only Supports Required (Wound Care, PEG Feeding)

Caller Type & Role Intake Nurse (Registered Nurse) calling participant/caregiver requiring clinical supports.

Context: Clarify clinical tasks, timing, protocols, supplies, and preferences. Convey competence and empathy; plan contingencies.

Key Information to Gather

  • Task specifics (wound type/dressing; PEG/PEJ regimen; catheter/injections).
  • Frequency and exact times; align to meals/meds where relevant.
  • Medical background and orders; discharge instructions.
  • Supplies and consumables; ordering and funding pathways.
  • Home setup: clean surface, pump/pole, sharps disposal, PPE.
  • Pain/comfort; red‑flag symptoms and current management.
  • Family involvement; training interest for gradual handover.
  • Contingencies (blocked/dislodged tube, worsening wound, late nurse).
  • NDIS high‑intensity lines; qualified staff assignment; continuity.

Common Concerns / Responses

“Will the nurse be skilled in my exact task?”
Yes — we allocate nurses experienced in your care area, with senior RN oversight and backup.
“Home isn’t like hospital — is it safe?”
We bring essentials, maintain sterility, and follow hospital‑grade protocols at home using your equipment or prescribed alternatives.
“Can I have the same nurse?”
Continuity is standard: a primary nurse plus one briefed backup — not a parade of new faces.
“What if something changes or an emergency occurs?”
We monitor, liaise with GP/specialists, and provide a 24/7 contact. For PEG issues, we’ll troubleshoot or direct you promptly to ED if required.

Suggested Next Steps

  • Book an initial nursing assessment aligned to first task (e.g., morning PEG feed).
  • Confirm orders and set up home charting (paper/electronic).
  • Educate and, if desired, gradually teach steps to increase independence.
  • Schedule efficient visits; combine tasks safely where possible.
  • Arrange consumables and track stock; provide direct nurse line.
  • Review after 4–6 weeks to taper or adjust plan.
Scripted Dialogue (PEG Example)

Intake Nurse: Hello [Participant], I’m [Name], a nurse with [Your Org]. I understand you have a PEG and need support with feeds and site care — could you walk me through your current routine?

Participant: Three pump feeds (8 AM, 1 PM, 7 PM) with flushes; I’m nervous to manage alone.

Intake Nurse: We’ll attend at those times, manage feeds/site care/meds, and — if you wish — teach steps gradually. You’ll have a 24/7 contact for issues like clogs or dislodgement. We’ll verify medication orders on the first visit and keep continuity with a primary nurse.
